# Trademark Registration

## What it is

Registering a brand name, logo, slogan, or other mark under the Trade Marks
Act, 1999 gives exclusive legal rights to use that mark nationally for the
goods/services it's registered under, and lets the owner take legal action
against infringers. Registration is not mandatory to use a mark, but an
unregistered mark has much weaker legal protection and is vulnerable to
being registered first by someone else.

## Fee structure (per class, per mark, online filing)

- **₹4,500** — Individuals, sole proprietors, DPIIT-recognised Startups, and
  Udyam (MSME)-registered enterprises (a 50% concession vs. the standard
  rate).
- **₹9,000** — Private Limited Companies, LLPs, partnerships, trusts, and
  other entities that don't qualify for the concession.
- Physical/offline filing costs roughly 10% more than online filing for both
  tiers.
- Fees are charged **per class, per mark** — filing one brand name in two
  classes costs double; filing both a wordmark and a separate logo mark
  doubles it again.
- The concession requires providing a valid DPIIT Startup certificate or
  Udyam certificate **at the time of filing** — there's no retroactive
  application if filed without it.
- Government fees are non-refundable, even if the application is rejected
  or opposed.

## Classes

India follows the Nice Classification: 45 classes, where classes 1–34 cover
goods and 35–45 cover services. A business needs to identify every class
relevant to its actual goods/services — e.g., a food-and-beverage app might
need both a goods class (for packaged food, if applicable) and a services
class (for the software/platform, and for restaurant/food-service
activity). Filing the wrong class is largely uncorrectable after submission
— it typically requires a fresh application and fresh fee.

## Process (high level)

1. Conduct a trademark search (free self-search on the IP India website, or
   a paid professional search) to check for conflicting existing marks.
2. File Form TM-A online at ipindiaonline.gov.in with the mark
   representation, applicant details, and the goods/services description
   mapped to the correct class(es).
3. Attach Power of Attorney (Form TM-48) if filed through an agent/attorney,
   and the DPIIT/Udyam certificate if claiming the fee concession.
4. The Registrar examines the application; if there's no conflict, it's
   published in the Trade Marks Journal for opposition (typically a
   multi-month window).
5. If unopposed (or opposition is resolved in the applicant's favor), the
   mark proceeds to registration.
6. Total time from filing to registration is commonly cited as 6–24 months
   depending on objections/oppositions — this is highly variable and not
   something to promise a founder as a fixed timeline.

## Objections and oppositions

- If the examiner raises an objection (e.g., under Section 9 — descriptive
  marks, or Section 11 — conflict with an existing mark), the applicant
  must respond within a defined window (commonly cited as 30 days).
- If a third party formally opposes the published mark, this becomes an
  adversarial proceeding that can involve hearings — budget for
  professional representation if this happens.

## Renewal

Registration is valid for **10 years** from the filing date and must be
renewed (Form TM-R) before expiry to remain active. Late renewal within a
grace period attracts a surcharge; if the mark lapses entirely, restoration
is more expensive than timely renewal, and until restored the name is
vulnerable to being claimed by someone else.

## Common pitfalls

- Filing as an individual founder to get the concession rate, then trying
  to transfer the mark to the company later — this is a separate assignment
  process with its own cost and paperwork; decide the applicant entity
  before filing.
- Skipping the pre-filing search to save money — an uncleared mark that
  later faces opposition costs more in response/hearing fees than a search
  would have cost upfront.
- Registering only a name, not the logo (or vice versa) — each needs its
  own registration if both need protection.
- Missing the 10-year renewal date — the surcharge for late renewal is
  significant, and full lapse creates real risk of losing the brand name.

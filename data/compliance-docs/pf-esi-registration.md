# PF (Employees' Provident Fund) and ESI (Employees' State Insurance)

## What they are

Two separate statutory social-security schemes with separate governing
acts, separate regulators, and separate thresholds. Employers commonly
bundle them together operationally, but a compliance gap in one does not
imply a gap in the other.

- **EPF** — governed by the Employees' Provident Fund and Miscellaneous
  Provisions Act, 1952; administered by EPFO. A retirement savings scheme;
  employer and employee each contribute a percentage of wages.
- **ESI** — governed by the Employees' State Insurance Act, 1948;
  administered by ESIC. A health/social-security scheme funded by
  employer and employee contributions, providing medical, maternity,
  disability, and sickness benefits.

## Applicability thresholds

- **EPF**: mandatory once an establishment employs **20 or more** persons
  (counted across permanent, contractual, part-time, and apprentice workers
  on any day during the year). EPFO has explicitly confirmed there is
  **no** change to this 20-employee threshold, despite periodic reports
  suggesting a reduction to 10 — verify against the current EPFO
  notification if this matters for a filing decision.
  - **Once-covered-always-covered**: an establishment that crosses 20
    employees stays covered under EPF even if headcount later drops below
    20.
  - Voluntary registration is available below the threshold (requires
    consent of employer and employees); once voluntarily registered, the
    establishment is permanently covered.
- **ESI**: mandatory once a factory or (in ESI-notified areas) other
  establishment employs **10 or more** persons — some states (commonly
  cited: Maharashtra, Chandigarh) apply a **20-employee** threshold instead
  of 10. Only employees earning up to a defined monthly wage ceiling
  (commonly cited as ₹21,000/month, ₹25,000 for persons with disabilities —
  confirm current figure) are actually covered for contributions, even
  though the *employer's* registration obligation is based on total
  headcount, not just covered employees.
  - "Once-covered-always-covered" applies to ESI as well.
  - ESI applies only in ESIC-notified geographic areas — a business outside
    a notified area may not have an ESI obligation even above the headcount
    threshold; confirm notification status for the specific location.

## Registration timelines and process

- Both are typically registered through the Shram Suvidha Portal (unified
  labour compliance portal), with registration expected promptly after
  crossing the threshold (EPF registration is commonly described as due
  within about 1 month; ESI within about 15 days — confirm current
  statutory windows).
- EPFO issues an Establishment Code; ESIC issues an Employer Code and
  employees get Insurance Person (IP) numbers.

## Ongoing obligations once registered

- **EPF**: monthly Electronic Challan cum Return (ECR) filing, generally
  by the 15th of the following month; UAN generation for new employees.
- **ESI**: monthly contribution deposit, generally by the 15th of the
  following month; half-yearly returns; new-employee registration within a
  short window of joining; workplace accident reporting within 24 hours.

## Penalties for non-registration or delayed compliance

- Registration is applied **retrospectively** from the date the threshold
  was actually crossed — meaning back-dated contributions become payable,
  not just contributions from the (late) registration date.
- Interest on delayed contributions (commonly cited around 12% per annum
  for ESI) plus additional damages/penalty as a percentage of arrears.
- Criminal liability provisions exist under both Acts for wilful default or
  false statements, including possible imprisonment in serious cases —
  confirm current provisions before citing specific penalty ranges to a
  founder.

## Common pitfalls

- Counting only "core team" or full-time employees toward the threshold —
  contractual, part-time, and (in most readings) directly-supervised
  contract labour typically count too; apprentices under the Apprentices
  Act are commonly excluded, but confirm.
- Assuming a startup is exempt because it's early-stage — there's no
  startup-specific exemption from PF/ESI; DPIIT recognition does not change
  these thresholds.
- Not realizing ESI's wage ceiling only limits *which employees* are
  covered, not *whether the employer must register* — registration
  obligation is headcount-based.
- Deregistering after headcount drops — once-covered-always-covered applies
  to both schemes.
